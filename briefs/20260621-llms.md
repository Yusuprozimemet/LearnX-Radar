# LLMs
## Why it's on the radar today
LLMs have been widely discussed in articles and job posts, highlighting their use-cases and limitations. This interest is driven by the increasing adoption of LLMs in various industries and the need for developers to understand their potential and challenges.

## What's being discussed
The current conversation around LLMs revolves around their potential to accelerate development, but also the risk of creating a "Generative Monoculture" where teams rely too heavily on LLMs and lose diversity in their solutions [1]. Some developers are exploring the use of LLMs in specific domains, such as retention modeling and customer support [5]. Others are working on building tools and platforms that integrate LLMs, such as Codex Migrate, which helps migrate and repair local Codex sessions [3].

## What you'll learn
* How LLMs can accelerate development and the risks associated with relying too heavily on them
* How to use LLMs in specific domains, such as retention modeling and customer support
* How to integrate LLMs with other tools and platforms

## Core ideas
LLMs are probabilistic systems trained on historical work and tuned towards answers that people tend to reward. This makes them extraordinary accelerators when used well, but can also create an optimization paradox where teams gain immediate implementation velocity while becoming anchored to a consensus baseline that may be too average for the actual system [1]. To avoid this, developers need to be aware of the limitations of LLMs and use them in a way that complements human judgment and creativity.

For example, in the context of retention modeling, LLMs can be used to generate personalized messages and responses, but human developers need to review and refine these outputs to ensure they align with the company's goals and values [5]. Similarly, when integrating LLMs with other tools and platforms, developers need to consider the potential risks and limitations of relying too heavily on these models.

## Where it fits
LLMs are a key technology in the field of artificial intelligence and machine learning, and are increasingly being used in various industries, including customer support, marketing, and software development. Developers who want to work with LLMs need to have a strong foundation in programming languages such as Python, Java, or C++, as well as experience with machine learning frameworks and libraries.
